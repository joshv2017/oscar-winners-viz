What it takes to win an Oscar

Introduction and motivation

For almost a century, the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences, colloquially known as "The Academy," has been responsible for identifying the most talented actors and actresses based on their performances in films of the most recent year. Using data made available by the Academy itself and IMDb, this project seeks to gain insight into what it takes, exactly, to win an Oscar. Where are these actors and actresses from? How are they connected? And does being better connected in Hollywood make you more predisposed to catch the eye of the Academy?

Their humble beginnings

Where are these successful actors from? How has that geographic distribution changed over time? Is there a common denominator here? This visualization is meant to be an exploratory tool for users to dive deeper into these questions. Hover over a marker to see what actor is from that area of the world, and learn a little bit more about them. Filter by birth year of the actors. Has the geographic makeup of top actors and actresses changed over time?

Some things worth noting: Our nominees and winners are overwhelmingly from the United States and Europe, especially the U.K. We do not start to see many international nominees or winners from outside of Europe or the U.S. until the 20th century. Most of the nominees and winners are white. There is clustering around cinema hubs like NYC and LA.

The Hollywood cliques

Beyond getting a spatial feel for where actors are from, we can also test the hypothesis that Oscar-winning actors and actresses are simply just better connected than their merely Oscar-nominated counterparts.

The following network visualization is a way to represent this data. The network visualizes the ways in which the Oscar-nominated actors and actresses from the last decade have worked with one another, costarring in films, TV shows, and other productions.

Hover over a node to view the actor or actress it is meant to represent; smaller nodes represent actors and actresses with fewer Academy Awards, while larger nodes represent actors and actresses with more. Click on a node to view the immediate connections between that actor and others. Does the hypothesis that actors and actresses with more Oscar awards are better connected in Hollywood actually hold? Are actors and actresses with no Oscar wins generally less connected than their Academy Award-winning counterparts? Is this just because an Oscar winner is more likely to have produced more work, and therefore has a larger number of costars?

While the network visualization provides some physical intuition for how these actors and actresses are connected, it's difficult to ascertain quantitatively whether actors and actresses that are Academy Award-winners are better connected. These bar charts display which winners and nominees have the most appearances with other winners and nominees over the course of their career, quantifying the number of those connections. Understanding that older actors have, by default, more connections to other actors since they have starred in more films, we have normalized versions of these graphs showing the total number of connections divided by the total number of credited appearances. Additionally, the blue line on each of the graphs indicates the median number of connections, or ratio of connections to films for the normalized graphs, for all of the actors and actresses.

You can also filter by award type here, and select a range of birth years of the actor. Do the winners seem to be more connected to other successful actors than the nominees are? Are the actors that appear in the normalized graphs very different from the actors that appear in the unnormalized graphs?

Key takeaways

After playing around with the birthplace visualization, you may have come to one of the same conclusions that we did:

The Oscar-nominated actors and actresses of Old Hollywood were mostly American- and European-born, and we see residual effects of this trend today.

Parasite (2019) was the first foreign film to win the Academy Award for Best Picture in the history of the organization. There is a striking lack of international actors and actresses nominated for Oscars.

From both the network visualization and the series of barcharts, you may have also reached the conclusion that

The Oscar-nominated actors and actresses of recent years are not as interconnected as the Oscar-nominated actors and actresses of earlier years.

You may ask if actors that won Academy Awards in the 1950s, for instance, were better connected with other Oscar nominees of that decade because they were able to live out full lifelong careers, producing more movies and working with more people. This is debunked, however, by taking a closer look at the average number of Oscar-winning costars per film each actor has. This statistic has been on the decline in recent years, indicating that the frequency with which Oscar-nominated actors and actresses work with one another is generally decreasing.

Take a look at the barchart visualization, and compare the unnormalized graphs to the normalized graphs. You may notice that both the unnormalized winner and nominee graphs contain a mixed bag of men and women, while the normalized graphs feature more women than their unnormalized counterparts. This holds true as you brush through different time periods.

This means that Oscar-winning women are in fewer movies than Oscar-winning men

The average number of Oscar-nominated costars per film is calculated by dividing the total number of connections an Oscar nominee has by the total number of films the actor has been in. Since the number of connections is the same between men and women, but the average number of costars per film is higher for women than for men, that must mean that Oscar-winning women are in fewer films than their male counterparts. Movies are written for men; Oscar-winning women are simply in fewer films.

Finally,

Number of connections is not a good predictor of number of Oscar wins or number of Oscar nominations.

Our visualization project began with the hypothesis that better-connected actors and actresses are more likely to win Oscars than their less-connected counterparts, but based on the network visualization above, there isn't a clear connection between number of connections to a node and the number of Oscar wins an actor or actress has.

Looking ahead

This visualization project has hopefully given you a taste of just how exclusive and hard-to-please the Academy can be. The makeup of Oscar nominees since the Academy's inception has consisted mainly of white- and Western-centric actors and actresses, and there's a gender bias in that women are not featured in Oscar-nominated films as much as men are, further reflected in recent scandals on the wage gap between actors and actresses. This project, however, only begins to address these limitations for the Best Actor/Actress and Best Supporting Actor/Actress categories, and says nothing about the lack of diversity for others that work hard to produce such culturally significant films.

It's important to support efforts to integrate the Academy with those that were previously marginalized and underrepresented. Some ways to do that include changing the composition of the Academy itself, and to support efforts to diversify Hollywood. We should continue to press for widespread changes, as some of the Academy's previous efforts have effected less change that hoped.
